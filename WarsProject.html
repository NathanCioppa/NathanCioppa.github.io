America's Wars Project - Rations in the U.S. Military

Revolutionary War

In the Revolutionary War, American soldier's rations were supposed to include meat, bread, beans or peas, and oftentimes rum or beer. However, soldiers would often not receive even half of what they were supposed to, since transporting food proved to be a challenge. Because of this issue, Washington allowed for local farmers to sell food to the soldiers, but most of them could not afford to buy food anyway. Another issue with rations at the time was that many foods would spoil quickly, so much of what soldiers were provided was salted or dry foods, which lasted much longer. Instead of regular bread, soldiers ate hardtack, which was essentially a very dense, hard cracker. Hardtack was cheap and lasted a long time, which made it the perfect food for the army.

Civil War

Union troops in the Civil War were given salt pork, coffee, and some sort of bread, which was usually hardtack. It is said that some of the hardtack that the soldiers ate was leftover from the Mexican-American war, and oftentimes had bugs in it. Soldiers would soak their hartack in coffee partially because that made it easier to eat, and partially because it would remove bugs from the hardtack by making them float to the surface of the coffee where soldiers could easily remove them. Confederate troops did not have access to coffee, and were much more likely to receive cornbread or cornmeal as opposed to wheat products, since corn was grown locally and was much more available.

World War I

American soldiers in the first world war ate similarly to soldiers in the civil war would have, with rations containing some sort of meat, hardtack, and coffee. During the war, Americans back home were encouraged to save food so that more could be sent to the soldiers, which would in turn help to win the war. There were various campaigns started to encourage americans to save food.

World War II

World War II saw the use of C-rations by the US military. C-rations came in many varieties, much different from how soldiers would have eaten in past wars. Some of those varieties include meat and spaghetti in tomato sauce, chopped ham, eggs and potatoes, meat and noodles, pork and beans, and chicken with vegetables. The rations also came with some sort of candy or chocolate, gum, biscuits, and cigarettes. The US military also experimented with another type of ration, known as the K-ration. The K-ration was lightweight, and not as nutritional as C-rations were. They were originally made for paratroopers as well as soldiers in emergency situations. K-rations did not provide enough calories for soldiers that used them over a long period of time, and their usage was discontinued after the war.

Korean War and Vietnam War

Starting with the Korean War and continuing through the Vietnam War, soldiers ate a revised version of C-rations. This revised version was designed to continuously feed soldiers in combat, much like the purpose of K-rations had been. Unlike K-rations, the new C-rations were successfully able to sustain troops. Soldiers oftentimes complained about the rations being heavy, as they weighed about five pounds. New varieties were introduced and along with the main meal, the rations would often include powdered milk and coffee, a can of fruit, and once again cigarettes and gum.

Modern Wars

The modern military uses MREs, which stands for Meal Ready-to-Eat. MREs contain about 1200 calories, and include a large variety of meals. Each includes a main course, side dish, dessert or snack, crackers or bread, and a beverage. In addition to the actual meal, the MRE contains utensils or (more commonly, just a single spoon), a flameless ration heater, and basic seasonings. Introduced in 2005, the vegetable cheese omelet MRE has become known as the worst MRE ever issued by the military. Soldiers complained not only about its taste, but even just the way it looked. The vegetable cheese omelet MRE was discontinued in 2009.

MREs have very strict packaging requirements. They must be able to withstand parachute drops from 1250 feet, and 98 foot drops without a parachute. They must also be able to maintain a shelf life of about 3 years if kept within an appropriate temperature range. Each weighs anywhere from 18 to 26 ounces. Although modern MREs are much better than previously used rations, the military is expected to provide soldiers with fresh food at least once every 21 days, but preferably more often than that.
